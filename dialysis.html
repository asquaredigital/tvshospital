Dialysis

What is Dialysis?

Dialysis cleans the blood when the kidneys can’t. It removes waste, extra water, and toxins so the body stays balanced. People with severe kidney problems use dialysis until their kidneys recover or they get a transplant.

Types of Dialysis

  • Hemodialysis: Blood is taken out, cleaned in a machine (an “artificial kidney”), then returned to the body.
  • Peritoneal dialysis: A special fluid is put into the belly. The belly lining acts as a filter to remove waste and extra water. The fluid is later drained out.

Hemodialysis: What to Expect

  1. Before starting: Weight, blood pressure, pulse, and temperature are checked. The skin near the access site (usually the arm) is cleaned.
  2. Needles: Two needles go into the access—one sends blood to the machine, the other returns the cleaned blood.
  3. Cleaning: Blood passes through a filter that removes wastes and extra water, then goes back into the body.
  4. Monitoring: Blood pressure and heart rate are watched during the session.
  5. Time: Usually 3–4 hours, done three times a week (your schedule may vary).
  6. After: Needles are removed, pressure is applied to stop bleeding, and weight may be checked again.
  7. Comfort: You can read, watch TV, or rest. Some people feel tired, dizzy, or have muscle cramps from fluid changes.

In short: Dialysis works like an artificial kidney to keep your blood clean and your body in balance when your kidneys can’t.
